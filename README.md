# Global Tuberculosis Burden Analysis (1990–2013)
### Descriptive Analysis Report
**Prepared by:** Mine Dema  
**Dataset:** WHO Global Tuberculosis Burden by Country  
**Tools Used:** Microsoft Excel (Data Cleaning) | Power BI (Visualization)

---

## 1. Overview

This analysis explores global tuberculosis (TB) burden across 218 countries and territories over a 24-year period (1990–2013). The dataset, sourced from the World Health Organization (WHO), contains key indicators including TB incidence, mortality, prevalence, and case detection rates. The goal is to identify global trends, high-burden regions and countries, and patterns in case detection that may inform public health understanding.

---

## 3. Global TB Incidence Trend

The global average TB incidence rate (per 100,000 population) rose steadily from **128.35 in 1990**, peaking at **148.61 in 2002**, before declining consistently to **112.96 in 2013**.

**Key Observation:** The peak around 2000–2002 coincides with the height of the HIV/AIDS epidemic, which significantly amplified TB vulnerability in many countries — particularly in Sub-Saharan Africa. The subsequent decline reflects the impact of global health initiatives such as the WHO's DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course) strategy and increased international funding for TB control.

> The overall incidence declined by approximately **24% from peak (2002) to 2013** — a meaningful improvement, though the burden remains significant.

## 4. Regional Analysis

| Region | Avg TB Incidence (per 100k) | Avg Mortality (per 100k) | Avg Detection Rate (%) | Countries |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| AFR (Africa) | 307.88 | 45.89 | 47.49% | 47 |
| SEA (South-East Asia) | 239.64 | 53.73 | 51.59% | 11 |
| WPR (Western Pacific) | 132.93 | 21.11 | 63.54% | 36 |
| EMR (Eastern Mediterranean) | 95.07 | 16.30 | 68.39% | 22 |
| EUR (Europe) | 60.61 | 5.22 | 74.51% | 54 |
| AMR (Americas) | 42.47 | 4.86 | 61.55% | 48 |

**Key Observations:**
- **Africa (AFR)** carries the highest TB incidence rate — more than **7 times** that of the Americas (AMR).
- **South-East Asia (SEA)** records the highest average mortality rate at 53.73 per 100,000 — higher even than Africa — despite having only 11 countries. This is driven by densely populated nations like India and Bangladesh.
- **Europe (EUR)** has the lowest burden and the highest case detection rate (74.51%), suggesting stronger healthcare infrastructure.
- Africa's case detection rate of **47.49%** is the lowest of all regions, meaning more than half of TB cases in Africa go undetected — a critical public health gap.

## 7. Countries With the Most Improvement

Comparing average incidence in the early period (1990–1995) vs the later period (2008–2013):

**Most Improved:**
- United Arab Emirates: **-79.72%**
- Andorra: **-78.83%**
- Slovakia: **-74.88%**
- Azerbaijan: **-73.85%**
- Slovenia: **-72.95%**

**Most Worsened:**
- Swaziland: **+367.49%**
- South Africa: **+208.88%**
- Lesotho: **+195.03%**
- Marshall Islands: **+198.18%**

**Key Observation:** The countries that worsened most dramatically are largely those hit hardest by the HIV/AIDS epidemic during the same period. Swaziland's TB incidence increased by over 367% — one of the starkest public health deteriorations in the dataset. This underscores how interconnected TB and HIV are as public health crises.

## 8. Case Detection Rate Analysis

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Global Average Detection Rate | 62.24% |
| Countries with Detection > 80% | 63 |
| Countries with Detection < 30% | 18 |

**Key Observation:** Globally, only about **62 in every 100 TB cases** were being detected on average across this period. This means nearly 4 in 10 cases went undiagnosed — representing a significant gap in TB control. Countries with lower detection rates tend to also have higher incidence, suggesting that undetected cases continue to spread the disease within communities.

**Total recorded TB deaths (excluding HIV-positive cases): ~35 million over 24 years.**

South-East Asia accounts for the highest total deaths, driven by the large populations of India, Bangladesh, and Indonesia. While Africa has a higher per-capita incidence rate, South-East Asia's sheer population size translates to higher absolute death counts.

---

## 10. Key Takeaways

1. **Global TB burden peaked around 2002 and has been declining** — a positive trend driven by international health interventions.
2. **Africa and South-East Asia bear the heaviest burden** — both in incidence and mortality.
3. **The TB-HIV co-infection crisis devastated Southern Africa** — countries like Swaziland, Lesotho, and South Africa saw incidence increase by over 200% during the period.
4. **Case detection remains a critical gap** — with a global average of only 62.24%, many cases go undiagnosed, fuelling continued transmission.
5. **Wealthier regions (Europe, Americas) show lower burden and higher detection rates** — highlighting the role of healthcare infrastructure in TB control.
6. **Progress is possible** — countries like UAE, Slovakia, and Azerbaijan reduced their TB incidence by over 70%, showing that strong health systems and targeted interventions can make a real difference.

---

## 11. Limitations

- Data covers 1990–2013 only — trends after 2013 are not reflected.
- Some values are WHO estimates, not exact counts, particularly for countries with limited reporting infrastructure.
- HIV-positive TB mortality data had significant missing values and was excluded from this analysis.
